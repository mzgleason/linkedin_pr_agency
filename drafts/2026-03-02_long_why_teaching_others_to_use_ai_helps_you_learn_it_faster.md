# Why teaching others to use AI helps you learn it faster

Last week I spent time demoing Codex for product and creative teams during an onsite — and the lesson that kept surfacing was simple: helping others learn a new tool forces you to clarify what actually matters. I started by building a few repeatable skills to share with colleagues. Preparing those demos exposed gaps in my own mental model faster than tinkering alone ever does.

A few practical observations from the week: AI makes it much easier to move on multiple projects, but my real bottleneck wasn’t the models — it was my ability to context-switch between them. That tension pushed me to refine an application I use to improve communication between executives and engineers so decisions and status are clearer. I also ran into production realities: my small PR agency workflow failed midweek (two of three posts sent early when the pipeline reset), which was a blunt reminder that end-to-end testing matters even when outputs look trivial.

Why this matters for product teams: teaching forces you to identify guardrails, define realistic expectations, and prioritize the smallest useful automations. When you do that, you can move from prototyping to measurable impact — something I’ve seen across my work, whether improving digital experiences, driving cross-sell growth, or saving significant operational losses. Those outcomes came from clear problem framing and disciplined execution, not from treating AI as a magic button.

If you’re starting to share AI learnings with your team, try treating your first sessions as experiments to expose assumptions, not to demonstrate perfection. You’ll learn faster — and your colleagues will get practical, repeatable skills they can adopt right away.
