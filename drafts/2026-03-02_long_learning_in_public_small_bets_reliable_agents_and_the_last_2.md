# Learning in public: small bets, reliable agents, and the last 20%

I’ve spent this week treating myself like a product team: building small, repeatable automations and treating them as experiments. On my laptop I connected Telegram to a personal assistant so I can create a ticket when I’m on a walk or don’t want to stop what I’m doing. I also have an automated “LinkedIn PR agency” that pings me with an interview script at lunch on Friday, then turns my answers into a storyboard and draft posts for the next week. And I’ve started a directory project for local dance studios that still needs more work on data scraping.

Three practical patterns stood out:
- Start tiny. The simpler the task you automate, the easier it is to build a robust test and verify that the agent still works as you iterate.
- Expect distraction. Agents help push projects forward even when you’re not paying attention, which is useful—and a productivity trap. The last 20% of a feature or automation still takes the longest and demands focused attention.
- Make tests part of the automation. When an agent acts on your behalf, the confidence bar should be higher: small, repeatable checks beat large, flaky heuristics.

These practices map back to how I think about integrating AI into product workflows at scale: rapid iteration, strong guardrails, and clear ownership. Across 8+ years in fintech, e-commerce, and platform businesses — currently as a Senior Product Manager in Charlotte — I’ve focused on applying data and product rigor to deliver measurable outcomes (for example, improved digital experience that increased engagement by 40%, recovering ~$9M in annualized losses from a routing fix, and driving cross-sell growth). Those wins matter, but the work here is more tactical: how to ship reliable automations that free up attention rather than fragment it.
